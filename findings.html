Chinese Nationalism in Early Twentieth-Century Textbooks

Findings

This page uses one focused example to show how textbook narratives changed in the early twentieth century. By comparing descriptions of the Yellow Emperor in an earlier textbook, in the Shiji, and in a 1928 textbook, students can see how an older political and civilizational narrative could be reworked into a more explicit nationalist one.

Questions for Comparison

  1. What kind of figure is the Yellow Emperor in each text: a ruler, a culture hero, or an ancestor?
  2. What is emphasized most strongly: political order, civilizational development, or ethnic origin?
  3. How does each text describe the people connected to the Yellow Emperor?
  4. What does each passage suggest about who “China” is and where Chinese civilization comes from?

Translated Sources

The three excerpts below are arranged for direct comparison. Read them side by side and pay attention to differences in vocabulary, emphasis, and historical meaning.

Source Comparison 1: 1904 Textbook Passage

The Yellow Emperor, Xuanyuan, arose when the age of Shennong was in decline. The feudal lords encroached upon one another and oppressed the people. He therefore used military force to punish those who would not submit, and he captured Chiyou at Zhuolu.

In civil governance and in benefiting the people, there were writing, the cyclical calendar signs, calendrical calculation, musical regulation, clothing, caps, utensils, currency, medicine, and the teaching of sericulture. In military organization and ordering the state, there were battle formations and systems of governance.

Source Comparison 2: Shiji (Records of the Grand Historian, which represents traditional narrative) Passage

In the time of Xuanyuan, the age of the Shennong clan had declined. The feudal lords invaded one another and cruelly oppressed the common people, while the Shennong clan was unable to subdue them. Xuanyuan thereupon trained in the use of arms, cultivated virtue, revitalized the army, planted the five grains, pacified the people, and brought order to the four directions.

Chiyou stirred up disorder and refused the emperor’s command. The Yellow Emperor therefore mobilized the feudal lords and fought Chiyou on the plain of Zhuolu, where he captured and killed him.

Source Comparison 3: 1928 Textbook Passage

Xuanyuan made boats, chariots, palaces, houses, upper garments, lower garments, bows, arrows, and characters. Before this, the ancient people’s clothing, food, and shelter were in a bitter condition. Only by the time of the Yan Emperor and the Yellow Emperor did civilization begin to develop.

Because both the Yan Emperor and the Yellow Emperor are the ancestors of the Han Chinese, it can be seen that Chinese civilization was initiated by the Han Chinese.

Interpretation and Limits

Read together, these passages show an important shift in emphasis. The earlier textbook passage remains close to the logic of the Shiji: the Yellow Emperor is primarily a sage ruler and culture hero who restores order, defeats rebellion, and establishes the foundations of civilized life. The narrative is political and civilizational before it is explicitly ethnic.

By contrast, the 1928 textbook does something more specific. It does not simply praise the Yellow Emperor as a founding ruler. It identifies the Yan Emperor and the Yellow Emperor as the ancestors of the Han Chinese and uses that claim to explain the origins of Chinese civilization. In this formulation, civilization is no longer only the product of good rulership or cultural development. It is also presented as the achievement of a named ethnic-national community.

This comparison helps students see that older historical narratives were not simply discarded. They were reused and reframed. A classical ruler could become a national ancestor, and a story about order and civilization could become a story about ethnic origin and collective identity.

This page uses one example to illustrate a larger argument. One comparison cannot represent all textbooks from the late Qing and Republican periods, and the shift from older political narratives to nationalist narratives was not uniform. Still, this case offers a clear way to show how inherited historical figures could be reinterpreted for new political purposes.
