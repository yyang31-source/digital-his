How Textbooks Taught New Ideas of the Chinese Nation

This site introduces undergraduate students to the changing language of nationalism in Chinese textbooks from the early twentieth century. By comparing a textbook from 1904 with one from 1928, the project shows how textbooks became a medium for teaching new ideas about the nation, citizenship, and collective identity.

Research Question

How did Chinese textbooks shift from older political narratives toward more explicit nationalist narratives in the early twentieth century, and what does that change reveal about the making of modern citizens?

Overview and Context

This project compares two Chinese textbooks published in 1904 and 1928. Rather than treating textbooks as neutral educational tools, it approaches them as historical sources that helped shape how students understood the political community they belonged to. The comparison makes it possible to trace how concepts of state, people, and history were reorganized during a period of major political and intellectual transformation.

In the late Qing and early Republican periods, schooling played an increasingly important role in forming modern subjects. Textbooks did not simply transmit information. They also organized historical knowledge, political values, and ideas about belonging in ways that taught students how to imagine China and their place within it.

Why Textbooks Matter

Textbooks are especially useful for studying modern nationalism because they were designed to be repeatable, authoritative, and widely circulated. They condensed complex political ideas into a teachable form for young readers. In that sense, textbooks were not only educational materials but also instruments for producing new kinds of citizens.

Looking closely at textbook language allows us to see how nationalism entered everyday learning. Instead of appearing only in elite political speeches or bureaucratic documents, it became part of ordinary classroom instruction.

Findings and Arguments

The comparison suggests a gradual but significant narrative shift. The earlier textbook remains closer to an older political mode of explanation, in which history is organized around rulers, dynasties, and state order. The later textbook moves more clearly toward a nationalist framework, emphasizing the nation as a collective subject and presenting history in terms of shared identity, political community, and national survival.

On the Findings page, you will see materials that helps to see this transformation.

What Students Can Learn

This site is designed for students in an introductory course on modern Chinese history. It invites readers to think about nationalism not as a fixed or timeless idea, but as something that had to be taught, repeated, and made legible through institutions such as schools and textbooks.

By the end of the site, students should be able to identify how educational materials participated in the making of modern political identity, and how changes in textbook language reflected broader transformations in early twentieth-century China.
