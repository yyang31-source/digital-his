How Early Twentieth-Century Chinese Textbooks Taught the Idea of Minzu

Research Question

How was the term minzu 民族 used across different school subjects in late Qing and Republican-era Chinese textbooks, and what can its frequency and surrounding context tell us about the construction of national identity as school knowledge?

This project examines how the word minzu 民族 appeared in early twentieth-century Chinese textbooks across different subjects, including history, geography, civics or morality, Chinese readers, and natural science. Rather than treating nationalism as a single fixed idea, the project asks how one important keyword moved through different areas of the modern school curriculum.

The project uses two digital methods: keyword frequency analysis and KWIC analysis. First, it counts how often minzu appears in textbooks from different subjects and normalizes the results by occurrences per 10,000 characters. Second, it uses KWIC, or Key Word in Context, to examine the words and phrases surrounding each occurrence of minzu. This makes it possible to ask not only where the term appeared most often, but also how its meaning changed across disciplinary settings.

By focusing on one keyword across multiple subjects, this project shows that minzu was not simply transmitted as a stable political concept. Instead, it was shaped by the specific knowledge forms of different school subjects.

Why Textbooks Matter

In the late Qing and Republican periods, textbooks became important tools for organizing modern knowledge and forming new kinds of students and citizens. They did not merely present information; they taught young readers how to understand history, territory, society, morality, and the political community to which they belonged.

Textbooks are especially useful for studying the development of national identity because they translated complex political concepts into teachable classroom language. A term such as minzu could appear in many different settings: as a historical subject in a history textbook, as a population category in a geography textbook, as an object of moral duty in a civics textbook, as an emotional identity in a reader, or as a racial or scientific category in a natural science textbook.

Looking across subjects allows us to see how nationalism entered everyday education not through one single narrative, but through a wider system of school knowledge.

Method and Argument

This project focuses on one core keyword: minzu. The choice is intentionally narrow. Instead of tracing a broad set of related terms such as 国家, 国民, 中华, 种族, or 人种, the project asks how the same word functioned differently depending on disciplinary context.

Frequency analysis identifies the relative density of minzu across different subjects. This helps show whether the word appeared more prominently in history, geography, civics, readers, or natural science textbooks. KWIC analysis then provides the surrounding context of each occurrence, allowing the project to interpret how the term was being used.

The central argument is that minzu was constructed differently across the curriculum. History textbooks may have historicized the term by connecting it to origins, dynasties, foreign invasion, heroes, and national survival. Geography textbooks may have spatialized it through population, territory, frontier, and ethnic distribution. Civics and morality textbooks may have moralized it through duty, sacrifice, patriotism, and responsibility. Chinese readers may have emotionalized it through suffering, spirit, compatriots, and national humiliation. Natural science textbooks may have racialized or biologized it by linking it to race, human type, physique, evolution, or competition.
