Teaching Chinese Nationalism in the Early Twentieth Century: Minzu Across School Subjects

This project follows one keyword, minzu (民族), through textbooks from the 1930s, a period when Chinese nationalism intensified under the pressure of Japanese aggression. In this context, cultivating national consciousness from childhood helped prepare students to become targets of later nationalist mobilization. This project therefore selects primary and secondary school textbooks from different subjects in the 1930s to explore how nationalism was taught to children through different forms of education.

Research Question

How did textbooks from different school subjects use minzu, and what do those differences reveal about how Chinese nationalism was taught as school knowledge?

Method

I selected five school subjects from the period: history, geography, civics, language readers, and self-cultivation. For each subject, I chose five textbooks, creating a sample of 25 books. This sample can be expanded further if more time and sources are available.

The project first uses keyword frequency analysis to count how often minzu appears in the twenty-five selected textbooks. This shows that the frequency of minzu differs clearly across subjects. Those differences require further interpretation, so I also use KWIC, or Key Word in Context, to locate the text before and after each occurrence of minzu. These KWIC tables help explain the specific contexts in which minzu appears and how the term was constructed differently across subjects.

Limitation

Compared with a traditional close-reading approach, this method makes keyword evidence more complete: every occurrence of minzu, along with its surrounding context, can be located and presented in a clear table. This makes it possible to see patterns that would be difficult to show by selecting only a few passages as examples.

At the same time, textbooks are historical objects. Where they were published, which schools used them, and how teachers taught them could all affect how students actually encountered the text. Therefore, this project involves a tradeoff: quantitative analysis can make keyword patterns more visible and complete, but questions about how those ideas were received, taught, and circulated still require traditional historical research.

Finding

The results suggest a division of knowledge in the construction of Chinese nationalism. The frequency of minzu differs sharply across subjects: history and civics contain the most frequent uses, while geography, language readers, and self-cultivation use the term less than half as often as the first two subjects. KWIC context helps explain this difference. History and civics are the most direct sites because they teach the national past, national belonging, and the ways students were asked to understand civic identity through minzu.

Geography, language readers, and self-cultivation mention minzu less often, but that lower frequency points to a different curricular role. These subjects did not always attach their content directly to the keyword, yet they still taught children to understand space, emotion, and moral order through nationalist narratives. In this sense, nationalism became pervasive across school knowledge: it was not confined to explicitly political lessons, but appeared as a broader way for students to interpret the world around them.
