Teaching Chinese Nationalism in the Early Twentieth Century: Minzu Across School Subjects

This project follows one keyword, minzu (民族), through five kinds of school textbooks. It asks how nationalism was taught not only through political language, but also through history, civics, geography, language readers, and self-cultivation.

The project focuses on textbooks from the 1930s, a period when Chinese nationalism intensified under the pressure of Japanese aggression. In this context, school textbooks did more than transmit subject knowledge. They also helped students imagine what China was, who belonged to it, and what responsibilities modern national subjects should carry.

Instead of treating nationalism as a single fixed idea, this site looks at how one keyword moved across different parts of the curriculum. The word minzu could name a people, a nation, a racial or ethnic group, or a collective historical community. Its meaning becomes clearer when we read both frequency patterns and surrounding context.

Research Question

How did textbooks from different school subjects use minzu (民族), and what do those differences reveal about how Chinese nationalism was taught as school knowledge?

Method and Limitation

I selected a balanced sample of 25 textbooks: five books each from history, civics, geography, language readers, and self-cultivation. This sample is intentionally limited but expandable. It is designed to compare subject emphasis rather than to represent every textbook used in Republican China.

The analysis combines keyword frequency and KWIC, or Key Word in Context. Frequency counts show where 民族 appears more often; KWIC examples show how the word is used in context. Because OCR and exact keyword searching cannot capture every related idea, the numbers are treated as an entry point for interpretation, not as the whole argument.

Finding

The five subjects taught national consciousness from different directions. History emphasized collective struggle and national crisis. Civics connected 民族 to political membership and social responsibility. Geography made the nation legible through territory, resources, and borders. Language readers taught national feeling through prose and exemplary writing. Self-cultivation linked national life to family order and moral duty.
