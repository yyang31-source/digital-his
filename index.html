How Early Twentieth-Century Chinese Textbooks Taught the Idea of Minzu

This project follows one keyword, minzu (民族), across five school subjects. It asks how a modern vocabulary of peoplehood, nation, and collective identity moved through history, civics, geography, language readers, and self-cultivation textbooks.

Explore the findings

Research Question

How was the term minzu (民族) used across different school subjects in late Qing and Republican-era Chinese textbooks, and what can its frequency and surrounding context tell us about the construction of national identity as school knowledge?

Corpus and Method

This project begins with textbooks from the 1930s, a period when Chinese nationalism intensified under the pressure of Japanese aggression. I selected a balanced sample of 25 textbooks: five books each from history, civics, geography, language readers, and self-cultivation. The sample is intentionally limited but expandable; its purpose is to test how the same keyword moved across different parts of the school curriculum.

I first count exact appearances of 民族 and normalize the result as occurrences per 10,000 cleaned analysis characters. I then use KWIC, or Key Word in Context, to read the passages around each occurrence. The frequency table shows where the term was concentrated; the KWIC examples show what the term was doing in each subject.
